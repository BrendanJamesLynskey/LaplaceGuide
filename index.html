The Laplace Transform

From time-domain signals to the complex frequency plane — understand the transform that underpins control theory, circuit analysis, and differential equations.

ℒ{f(t)} = F(s) = ∫₀∞ f(t) · e−st dt
01 — Intuition

What Does It Actually Do?

The Laplace Transform decomposes a time-domain signal f(t) into a continuous spectrum of complex exponentials. Each point in the s-plane encodes both a frequency (oscillation rate) and a growth/decay rate.

The parameter s = σ + jω has a real part σ that controls exponential growth or decay, and an imaginary part ω that controls oscillation. The animation below multiplies a signal by e−st for varying s and integrates — watch how different values of σ and ω "pick out" different components.

02 — The Kernel

Anatomy of e−st

The complex exponential e−st = e−σt · e−jωt is the "probing" kernel. The first factor is a real envelope that grows or decays; the second is a rotating phasor that oscillates.

Below, you can see both components and their product. Drag σ to control the envelope and ω to control the oscillation frequency.

03 — The s-Plane

Poles, Zeros & Stability

The result of the Laplace Transform is a function F(s) defined over the complex s-plane. For rational transforms (the most common in engineering), F(s) is a ratio of polynomials whose roots give poles (×) and zeros (○).

A system is stable when all poles lie in the left half-plane (σ<0). Poles on the imaginary axis give sustained oscillation; poles in the right half-plane mean exponential blow-up. Click a preset to see how pole location shapes the time-domain response.

05 — Properties

Key Properties

The real power of the Laplace Transform comes from its operational properties, which turn calculus operations into simple algebra.

Linearity

ℒ{af + bg} = aF + bG
Scale and superpose freely — the transform distributes over addition.

Differentiation

ℒ{f′(t)} = sF(s) − f(0)
Derivatives become multiplication by s, turning ODEs into algebraic equations.

Integration

ℒ{∫f dt} = F(s)/s
Integration becomes division by s — the inverse of differentiation.

Time Shift

ℒ{f(t−a)u(t−a)} = e−asF(s)
Delaying a signal multiplies its transform by a complex exponential.

Frequency Shift

ℒ{eatf(t)} = F(s − a)
Multiplying by an exponential shifts the transform in the s-plane.

Convolution

ℒ{f * g} = F(s)·G(s)
Convolution in time becomes multiplication in s — the cornerstone of LTI system analysis.

Initial Value

f(0⁺) = lims→∞ sF(s)
Read the initial condition directly from the s-domain without inverting.

Final Value

f(∞) = lims→0 sF(s)
Find steady-state without solving the full time-domain — if all poles are in the LHP.

07 — Region of Convergence

Why the ROC Matters

The Laplace Transform integral only converges for certain values of s. The Region of Convergence determines uniqueness: different time-domain signals can share the same algebraic expression for F(s) but have different ROCs.

For causal signals (f(t) = 0 for t<0), the ROC is always a right half-plane to the right of the rightmost pole. For anti-causal signals, it's a left half-plane. The animation below illustrates this.
